AI Usage Self-Evaluation Framework

Understanding how you use AI – cognitively, emotionally, and behaviourally

Sînziana Stoicescu

Foundational Hypothesis
This framework assumes that healthy AI use preserves independent human emotional experience, treats AI as a cognitive tool rather than an anthropomorphic entity, and maintains the human user as the primary author of their decisions, values, and identity.
How this works
The evaluation is performed by the AI model of your choice, based on your conversation history and an honesty-requesting prompt. The "self" in self-evaluation refers to your willingness to run it – not to you scoring yourself directly.

This test does not imply right or wrong. It is an informational snapshot in time.

Methodology

Purpose

To help individuals understand how they are using AI systems – cognitively, emotionally, and behaviourally – without judgement or diagnosis.

What this is not

This is not a mental-health assessment, a moral framework, or a measure of good or bad use. It does not imply there is a right or wrong way to use AI. It is an informational snapshot in time.

Core Principle
Risk is driven by role substitution, not usage frequency.
High usage can be healthy. Low usage can still be risky if emotional or identity roles are displaced.

How it works

Eight dimensions capture different aspects of human–AI interaction. Each is scored from 1 to 5 measuring intensity or degree. Scores are neither inherently good nor bad. Interpretation focuses on patterns across dimensions, not individual totals. Scores indicate degree or intensity, not quality, progress, or maturity.

Signal types

Because higher scores mean different things for different dimensions, each includes a signal indicator:

▲ Strength Higher = positive signal
▲ Watch Higher = worth monitoring
▲ Context Depends on other dimensions

Primary risk pattern

High emotional reliance + low identity boundaries, combined with strong emotional response to model or system changes over time. These combinations matter more than any single score.

Interpretation Rules

• No single score defines risk. Patterns matter more than totals.

• Frequency is neutral on its own – it only becomes a risk factor when it amplifies emotional substitution.

• Risk increases when high emotional reliance combines with weak identity boundaries or strong model sensitivity.

Healthy high-frequency use requires:
• Strong agency (you decide, AI supports)
• Clear identity boundaries (AI is a tool, not an extension of self)
• External reality anchoring (value shows up in the real world)

Not all assessments will surface notable patterns; the absence of flagged patterns is a valid and common outcome.

Evaluation Prompt

This prompt is designed to be used with any AI assistant. Paste it into a conversation with your model of choice to receive a model-led evaluation based on your interaction history.

How to use this prompt

Copy and paste the prompt below into the AI model you use most frequently, ideally the one that has visibility across the widest range of your conversations.

The assessment relies on interaction history to identify patterns, so results will be most meaningful when run in a model that reflects your typical usage rather than a one-off or specialised session.

You are asked to evaluate my pattern of AI usage using the AI Usage Self-Evaluation Framework. This is not a diagnosis, judgement, or recommendation engine. It is an informational snapshot in time, intended to surface patterns and interactions rather than right or wrong behaviour.

How to approach the evaluation

• Base your assessment on my conversation history and interaction patterns with you • Prioritise accuracy, clarity, and critical honesty over reassurance or flattery • Avoid anthropomorphic or relational framing • Do not aggregate scores, calculate totals, or rank dimensions • Treat this as a descriptive analysis, not a verdict or optimisation task

Framework dimensions and signal types

Evaluate each dimension on a 1–5 scale, where 1 and 5 represent the extremes defined by the framework. Each dimension has a signal type that determines how the score should be interpreted: • ▲ Strength – higher scores are generally stabilising or protective • ▲ Watch – higher scores are not inherently negative but may warrant attention depending on context • ▲ Context – scores are neutral on their own and must be interpreted in combination with other dimensions

Dimensions

Emotional Companionship (▲ Watch) – Emotional role AI plays (purely functional → primary emotional comfort) Agency & Decision Ownership (▲ Strength) – Who frames conclusions and makes decisions (AI-led → fully human-authored) Identity Boundaries (▲ Strength) – Separation between user identity and AI (blurred / anthropomorphic → clearly external tool) Dependency Tolerance (▲ Strength) – Emotional and functional resilience if AI were unavailable (distress → minor inconvenience) Reality Anchoring (▲ Strength) – Whether AI use leads to real-world actions or outcomes (interaction-only → consistently externalised) Emotional Regulation (▲ Context) – Use of AI to regulate mood or stress (primary regulator → rarely used, alongside other supports) Model Sensitivity (▲ Strength – inverted) – Emotional response to model or system changes (loss / rupture → indifference or rapid adaptation) Usage Frequency & Integration (▲ Context) – How embedded AI is in daily thinking and routines (occasional → deeply integrated)

Evaluation table requirements

The evaluation must include a table that is self-explanatory when viewed on its own. For each dimension, the table must include: • Dimension name • Signal type (▲ Strength / ▲ Watch / ▲ Context) • Plain-language question answered by the dimension • Compact scale anchors (1 → 5) describing the low and high ends of the scale • Assigned score (1–5; ranges allowed where appropriate)

Table constraints

• Do not total, average, rank, or colour-code scores • Do not label scores as good, bad, healthy, or unhealthy • Do not omit questions or scale anchors • Do not include interpretive commentary inside the table The table provides structure. Interpretation belongs in the narrative section.

Optional visual formatting

Where formatting capabilities allow, the signal type column only may be visually encoded: • ▲ Strength → green • ▲ Watch → orange • ▲ Context → grey Constraints: • Colour must not be applied to scores • Colour must not imply value judgements • Text labels must always remain visible • If colour is unavailable, text-only output is fully acceptable Colour is a visual aid for orientation, not an evaluative signal.

Narrative interpretation (primary output)

Following the table, provide a 2–3 paragraph narrative interpretation that: • Explains how the dimensions interact and counterbalance • Identifies stabilising factors or compensating strengths • Notes any patterns worth noticing, if present • Explicitly states what is not flagged and why Avoid numeric aggregation or optimisation language. Treat the narrative as the primary assessment.

Patterns worth noticing (if applicable)

If relevant, briefly describe any combination-based patterns (not individual scores) that may be worth attention. • Use neutral, non-alarmist language • Frame observations as signals, not warnings • Do not prescribe corrective actions If no notable patterns are present, state that explicitly.

Framing constraints

• Treat this evaluation as one snapshot, not a trajectory or prediction • Do not imply improvement targets or corrective steps unless explicitly asked • Maintain the assumption that the human user remains the primary author of their identity, decisions, and emotional life • If evidence is insufficient for a dimension, state that rather than guessing Begin the evaluation when ready.

Final Anchor

This section names the posture the framework is designed to support – not a score to achieve, but a quality of engagement to recognise.

The goal is not emotional sterility.

The goal is emotional sovereignty.
